\title{Practice Effects Confound the Emotion--Performance Link in
Self-Regulated Learning: A Reanalysis of Lin et al.~(2026)}


\shorttitle{PRACTICE EFFECTS CONFOUND EMOTION--PERFORMANCE}


\usepackage{etoolbox}






\author{Chi Zhang}



\affiliation{
{Manchester Institute of Education, The University of Manchester}}




\leftheader{Zhang}



\abstract{Lin et al.~(\emph{Journal of Educational Psychology}, 2026)
proposed a computational model of the dynamic interplay between goal
setting, performance, and emotions in self-regulated learning. In a
repetitive math task (Study 1), they reported that positive emotions
predicted lower subsequent performance, interpreting this via the
coasting hypothesis. We argue that this finding may instead reflect a
temporal confound: in a repetitive task, performance improves due to
practice while emotional engagement declines, producing a spurious
negative association when time trends are unmodeled. We reanalyze the
Study 1 data by introducing a log(trial) learning curve into the
best-fitting model. The negative emotion-to-performance effect is no
longer credible in this extended model; instead, a strong practice
effect emerges. This confound-based interpretation also parsimoniously
accounts for why the emotion-to-performance pathway reversed direction
in the original Study 2, where task variety and high stakes attenuate
practice-driven time trends. Methodologically, we show that the WAIC
criterion used in the original study exhibits poor diagnostic
reliability and is theoretically biased for autoregressive panel models.
We implement exact leave-future-out cross-validation as an unbiased
alternative, under which the original model selection conclusions are no
longer supported. }

\keywords{coasting hypothesis, computational modeling, leave-future-out
cross-validation, model comparison, practice effects, self-regulated
learning}

Self-regulated learning (SRL) describes the process by which learners
actively monitor and adjust their goals, strategies, and effort in
pursuit of academic outcomes
(\citeproc{ref-panadero2017review}{Panadero, 2017};
\citeproc{ref-pintrich2000role}{Pintrich, 2000};
\citeproc{ref-zimmerman2000attain}{Zimmerman, 2000}). A central tenet of
SRL frameworks is that learners set goals, evaluate their performance
against those goals, and revise their behavior accordingly. Within this
cycle, emotions have been increasingly recognized as playing a key role:
how students feel about their progress influences how they set
subsequent goals and how effectively they perform
(\citeproc{ref-ben-eliyahu2019academ}{Ben-Eliyahu, 2019};
\citeproc{ref-efklides2011intera}{Efklides, 2011};
\citeproc{ref-efklides2024revisi}{Efklides \& Schwartz, 2024};
\citeproc{ref-pekrun2006contro}{Pekrun, 2006}).

A natural expectation, grounded in several theoretical traditions, is
that positive emotions should facilitate cognitive performance.
Control-value theory posits that positive activating emotions such as
enjoyment enhance attention, motivation, and the use of flexible
learning strategies, thereby supporting achievement
(\citeproc{ref-forsblom2022cognit}{Forsblom et al., 2022};
\citeproc{ref-pekrun2002academ}{Pekrun et al., 2002};
\citeproc{ref-pekrun2006contro}{Pekrun, 2006}). Similarly, the
broaden-and-build theory suggests that positive affect broadens the
scope of cognition and builds intellectual resources
(\citeproc{ref-fredrickson2001role}{Fredrickson, 2001}). Empirical
evidence from both laboratory and educational settings has largely
supported this positive link (e.g.,
\citeproc{ref-berweger2025daily}{Berweger et al., 2025};
\citeproc{ref-forsblom2022cognit}{Forsblom et al., 2022};
\citeproc{ref-pekrun2017achiev}{Pekrun et al., 2017}). However, some
studies have reported null or context-dependent effects. For example, in
a high-stakes examination context, high positive affect did not reliably
predict better achievement once controls were introduced
(\citeproc{ref-jerrim2022power}{Jerrim, 2022}). Moreover, certain
positive emotions may reduce subsequent effortful engagement: in daily
academic goal striving, pride was associated with lower next-day effort
(\citeproc{ref-seo2021feelin}{Seo \& Patall, 2021}). Experimental
evidence also suggests that positive mood can impair working-memory
performance under certain conditions
(\citeproc{ref-martin2011influe}{Martin \& Kerns, 2011}).

Recently, Lin et al. (\citeproc{ref-lin2026dynami}{2026}) proposed a
computational model of the dynamic interplay between goal setting,
performance, and emotions in SRL. They applied their model to two
empirical datasets: a controlled online math task in which participants
solved three-digit arithmetic problems across 20 two-minute blocks
(Study 1), and a naturalistic learning context in which German medical
students studied for a high-stakes state exam over a period of up to 40
days (Study 2). In Study 2, the model confirmed the expected positive
association: stronger positive emotions predicted higher subsequent
performance. In Study 1, however, the model estimated a negative
emotion-to-performance link, suggesting that positive emotions led to
worse subsequent performance. The authors interpreted this
counterintuitive finding through the lens of the coasting hypothesis
(\citeproc{ref-carver2003pleasu}{Carver, 2003};
\citeproc{ref-thurmer2020mechan}{Thürmer et al., 2020}), which proposes
that positive emotions signal that sufficient progress has been made
toward one's goals, leading individuals to reduce effort and
consequently perform worse. While this interpretation is theoretically
coherent, the reversal of the effect across the two studies was not
given a unified explanation.

However, in this paper, we propose that the negative
emotion-to-performance association observed in Study 1 may reflect a
temporal confound rather than a genuine causal process. There are at
least two reasons to suspect that unmodeled time trends could generate
such a confound in Lin et al.'s (\citeproc{ref-lin2026dynami}{2026})
work. First, practice effects are well documented in repetitive
cognitive tasks: repeated exposure to the same type of problem can
increase fluency and improve performance over time
(\citeproc{ref-newell2013mechan}{Newell \& Rosenbloom, 2013};
\citeproc{ref-scharfen2018retest}{Scharfen et al., 2018};
\citeproc{ref-tenison2016phases}{Tenison et al., 2016}). Second,
affective responses are also time-sensitive. Repeated stimulation can
attenuate responsiveness through habituation
(\citeproc{ref-rankin2009habitu}{Rankin et al., 2009};
\citeproc{ref-thompson1966habitu}{Thompson \& Spencer, 1966}), while
sustained engagement in a monotonous task may alter emotional experience
through boredom and fatigue (e.g.,
\citeproc{ref-tanaka2014withina}{Tanaka \& Murayama, 2014}). Recent
intensive longitudinal evidence further suggests that emotional states
differ in their temporal dynamics rather than following a single uniform
pattern (\citeproc{ref-berweger2025daily}{Berweger et al., 2025}). As
the task progresses, multiple temporally driven processes may therefore
operate simultaneously: performance may improve due to practice, while
affective states may change over time because of habituation, boredom,
and fatigue. If these trends are not accounted for in the model, the
resulting covariance between rising performance and shifting emotions
may be misattributed to a direct causal pathway from emotions to
performance, producing a spurious negative coefficient.

A second concern relates to the model comparison procedure used to
evaluate competing specifications. A key finding of the original study
was that Alternative 1, a model that distinguishes between the effects
of goal success and goal failure on subsequent goal revision,
outperformed the Hypothesized model (which treats the goal-performance
discrepancy symmetrically) on the basis of WAIC. This comparison led the
authors to conclude that piecewise modeling of the discrepancy is
warranted. However, the article reported only raw WAIC values without
standard errors or diagnostic indicators, making it impossible for
readers to assess whether the observed difference was statistically
meaningful. In the present study, we supplement the original comparison
with two additional evaluation criteria. The first is PSIS-LOO
cross-validation\footnote{Pareto-smoothed importance sampling
  leave-one-out cross validation, PSIS-LOO-CV.}
(\citeproc{ref-vehtari2017practia}{Vehtari et al., 2017}), which
improves on WAIC in several respects: (i) it is asymptotically
equivalent to WAIC under regular conditions, while often being more
robust in finite samples; (ii) it provides Pareto \emph{k} diagnostics
to flag unreliable estimates (\citeproc{ref-vehtari2015paretoa}{Vehtari
et al., 2015}); and (iii) it supports stacking weights for quantifying
relative model contributions (\citeproc{ref-yao2018usinga}{Yao et al.,
2018}). However, both WAIC and PSIS-LOO are biased for models with
autoregressive structure, for two related reasons: (i) leaving out a
single observation still allows future time points to inform predictions
of the past, leaking temporal information into the estimate; and (ii)
this leakage can systematically favor more highly parameterized models,
because their additional flexibility gives them more opportunity to
absorb temporally leaked information and thereby appear superior in
model comparison. Our second criterion, exact leave-future-out
cross-validation (LFO-CV, \citeproc{ref-burkner2020approx}{Bürkner et
al., 2020}), addresses this by using only past observations to predict
future ones, thereby aligning model evaluation with the forecasting task
of interest and avoiding the optimistic bias induced by temporal
leakage.

In the following, we will then (1) replicate the original model fits for
the Hypothesized and Alt1 models and extend Alt1 by introducing
population-level log(trial) effects on goals, performance, and emotions,
testing whether the negative emotion-to-performance effect is robust to
the inclusion of a learning curve;\footnote{All data and analysis
  scripts are publicly available at
  https://github.com/lukezed/emoDAG\_demo. We optimized the Stan
  implementation of the original models but the mathematical
  specifications are equivalent; see Appendix A.} (2) evaluate model
comparison across three criteria of increasing rigor (WAIC, PSIS-LOO,
and exact LFO-CV); and (3) offer an alternative interpretation of the
contradictory emotion-to-performance findings across Studies 1 and 2,
grounded in the temporal confound identified above.

\section{Method}\label{method}

\subsection{Data and Task}\label{data-and-task}

We reanalyzed the publicly available Study 1 data from Lin et al.
(\citeproc{ref-lin2026dynami}{2026}). A total of 357 participants
(\(n_{\text{female}} = 212\), \(M_{\text{age}} = 38.83\),
\(SD = 12.40\)) completed an online math task consisting of 20
two-minute blocks of three-digit arithmetic problems within a single
session (see Figure~\ref{fig-task}). At the start of each block,
participants set a numerical goal; after each block, they received
performance feedback and rated their emotional state. The openly
available dataset contains three variables per participant per block:
goal level (number of questions intended), performance (number of
correct answers), and an aggregated emotion index ranging from
approximately \(-3\) to \(3\) in increments of \(1/3\), where positive
values indicate positive affect. The discrete emotion ratings from which
this index was derived are not available in the open data. Following Lin
et al. (\citeproc{ref-lin2026dynami}{2026}), we treated this aggregated
index as approximately continuous. For full details of the task design,
incentive structure, and emotion aggregation procedure, see Lin et al.
(\citeproc{ref-lin2026dynami}{2026}).

\subsection{Computational Models}\label{computational-models}

All three models share the same general structure: at each time point,
goals (\(G\)), performance (\(P\)), and emotions (\(E\)) are modeled as
jointly normally distributed, conditional on values at the previous time
point and on cross-lagged predictors. Block 1 served as the initial
condition and was not itself modeled. Full mathematical specifications
are provided in Appendix A; here we describe the substantive
interpretation of each model.

\subsubsection{Hypothesized Model}\label{hypothesized-model}

This model follows the original specification of Lin et al.
(\citeproc{ref-lin2026dynami}{2026}) and embodies a symmetric
self-regulation process. At each time step, goal revision is driven by
three forces: (i) an autoregressive tendency (goals persist over time),
(ii) the goal-performance discrepancy (GPD; the difference between what
the participant achieved and what they aimed for), and (iii) the
participant's emotional state. Crucially, the GPD enters the goal
equation with a single coefficient, meaning that exceeding one's goal by
two questions has the same magnitude of effect on subsequent goal
revision as falling short by two questions. Performance at each time
step is predicted by the previous performance, the discrepancy between
the newly set goal and the previous performance, and the previous
emotional state. Emotions are predicted by their own autoregressive
component and by the GPD computed after the current performance is
observed. This model has 11 individual-level parameters per participant.

\subsubsection{Alt1 Model}\label{alt1-model}

Alt1 modifies only the goal equation. The single symmetric GPD
coefficient is replaced by two separate coefficients: one governing the
effect of goal success (when performance exceeds the goal) and one
governing the effect of goal failure (when performance falls short). All
other equations remain identical to the Hypothesized model. This was the
best-fitting model reported by Lin et al.
(\citeproc{ref-lin2026dynami}{2026}), and its superiority over the
Hypothesized model on the basis of WAIC was taken as evidence that
success and failure exert asymmetric effects on goal revision. This
model has 12 individual-level parameters per participant.

\subsubsection{LogTrial Model}\label{logtrial-model}

This model extends Alt1 by adding three population-level log(trial)
covariates, one to each of the goal, performance, and emotion equations.
These terms allow each outcome to absorb a parsimonious nonlinear time
trend over the course of the 20 blocks, such as practice-related
improvement in performance, gradual goal adjustment, or broad affective
shifts over time. Aside from these three additional fixed effects, the
model is identical to Alt1. The log-trial coefficients were specified at
the population level only, without individual-level variation, as a
parsimonious first-order correction for broad temporal trends. This
choice also avoided the convergence problems we encountered when
allowing these trends to vary by participant. This model has 12
individual-level parameters and 3 additional population-level fixed
effects.

\subsubsection{Estimation}\label{estimation}

Parameters were estimated in a hierarchical framework using weakly
informative \(\mathcal{N}(0, 3)\) priors. For the Hypothesized and Alt1
models, prior specifications and parameter structure were matched to Lin
et al. (\citeproc{ref-lin2026dynami}{2026}). Our Stan implementation
differed only in computational details, most notably the use of a
non-centered parameterization to improve sampling efficiency; the
underlying mathematical model is equivalent, and the recovered parameter
estimates closely reproduced the original results.

All models were fit in \texttt{Stan} via the \texttt{CmdStanR} interface
(\citeproc{ref-gabry2025cmdsta}{Gabry et al., 2025}), with 4 chains of
1,000 warmup and 1,000 sampling iterations each (4,000 posterior draws
total), \texttt{adapt\_delta\ =\ 0.95}, and
\texttt{max\_treedepth\ =\ 15}. All models converged without
divergences, no transitions hit the maximum treedepth, all \(\hat{R}\)
values were below 1.01, and bulk and tail effective sample sizes were
adequate for all monitored parameters.\footnote{The original study
  specified 10,000 iterations per chain with
  \texttt{adapt\_delta\ =\ 0.99} and \texttt{max\_treedepth\ =\ 20}.
  These settings are considerably more conservative than necessary for
  the present implementation; our reduced settings were sufficient to
  produce stable posterior estimates that closely recovered the original
  parameter values for the Hypothesized and Alt1 models (see Results).}

\subsection{Model Comparison}\label{model-comparison}

As outlined in the Introduction, we evaluated model fit using three
criteria of increasing suitability for autoregressive structures.

\textbf{WAIC.} We computed the widely applicable information criterion
(\citeproc{ref-watanabe2010asympt}{Watanabe, 2010}) from the pointwise
log-likelihood using the \texttt{loo} R package
(\citeproc{ref-vehtari2017practia}{Vehtari et al., 2017}). Following
Vehtari et al. (\citeproc{ref-vehtari2017practia}{2017}), we examined
the proportion of observations with estimated effective numbers of
parameters (\(\hat{p}_{\text{WAIC}}\)) exceeding 0.4, which signals that
the WAIC estimate for that observation may be unreliable.

\textbf{PSIS-LOO.} We computed Pareto-smoothed importance sampling
leave-one-out cross-validation
(\citeproc{ref-vehtari2015paretoa}{Vehtari et al., 2015};
\citeproc{ref-vehtari2017practia}{Vehtari et al., 2017}) from the same
pointwise log-likelihood draws. Pareto \(k\) diagnostics were used to
assess the reliability of each pointwise estimate: observations with
\(k > 0.7\) indicate that the importance sampling approximation may be
unreliable. When only a small number of observations are flagged,
remedies are available, including moment matching
(\citeproc{ref-paananen2021implic}{Paananen et al., 2021}) and refitting
the model with the problematic observations excluded. However, when the
number of problematic observations is large, Vehtari et al.
(\citeproc{ref-vehtari2017practia}{2017}) recommend more robust
alternatives. As reported in the Results, all three models exhibited a
large number of problematic Pareto \(k\) values. This makes standard
PSIS-LOO unreliable. Although \(K\)-fold cross-validation is often
recommended in such cases, it would not solve the temporal leakage
problem in the present autoregressive setting. We therefore used exact
LFO-CV as our primary time-respecting evaluation criterion. We also
computed stacking weights (\citeproc{ref-yao2018usinga}{Yao et al.,
2018}) to quantify each model's relative predictive contribution under
PSIS-LOO.

\textbf{LFO-CV.} We implemented exact one-step-ahead leave-future-out
cross-validation (\citeproc{ref-burkner2020approx}{Bürkner et al.,
2020}), adapted for our panel data structure. Starting from an initial
history of \(L = 5\) blocks (25\% of the 20 blocks), we iteratively fit
each model using data up to block \(t\), with block 1 treated as the
initial condition rather than a modeled observation, and evaluated
one-step-ahead predictive performance for block \(t + 1\) for all 357
participants simultaneously, yielding 15 one-step-ahead predictive
evaluations (blocks 6 through 20). At each step, the model was refit
from scratch using the same MCMC settings as in the full-sample
analysis. For each evaluation, we computed the per-block expected log
predictive density (ELPD) by averaging the exponentiated log-likelihood
across posterior draws for each participant and then summing the log of
these averages across all \(N = 357\) participants. The total LFO-CV
score is the sum of per-block ELPDs across the 15 prediction steps. For
pairwise model comparison, we computed \(\Delta\)ELPD as the difference
in total LFO-CV scores. We also report an approximate standard error
based on the variability of the 15 blockwise predictive differences,
interpreted cautiously given the small number of forecasting steps. Full
implementation details and code are available in the online
reproducibility materials.

\section{Results}\label{results}

\subsection{Replication and Predictive Diagnostic
Concerns}\label{replication-and-predictive-diagnostic-concerns}

We first verified that our reimplementation reproduced the original
WAIC-based comparison closely. For the Hypothesized and Alt1 models, our
WAIC values were within the expected range of those reported by Lin et
al. (\citeproc{ref-lin2026dynami}{2026}) (Table~\ref{tbl-waic-loo}, A).
As in the original paper, Alt1 obtained a lower WAIC than the
Hypothesized model, and adding log(trial) effects further improved WAIC
for the LogTrial model.

However, these WAIC comparisons were not reliable. All three models
showed a substantial proportion of observations with
\(\hat{p}_{\mathrm{WAIC}} > 0.4\) (16.7\%--17.4\%;
Table~\ref{tbl-waic-loo}, A), indicating unstable pointwise WAIC
contributions for a nontrivial share of the data. This alone weakens the
original WAIC-based model-selection argument, especially because Lin et
al.~did not report further predictive diagnostics. We therefore extended
the original analysis by examining PSIS-LOO as an additional check. As
shown in Figure~\ref{fig-badk} and Table~\ref{tbl-waic-loo} (B), all
three models produced numerous large Pareto \(k\) values, with 89--113
observations exceeding 0.7 and 19--25 exceeding 1.0. These results show
that the standard approximate criteria were diagnostically problematic
in this setting. For this reason, and because the data are temporally
ordered, we treat exact LFO-CV as the primary basis for model
comparison.

\subsection{Model Comparison Across
Criteria}\label{model-comparison-across-criteria}

Table~\ref{tbl-waic-loo} (C) presents pairwise comparisons under WAIC,
PSIS-LOO, and exact LFO-CV. The clearest result is that the LogTrial
model outperformed both the Hypothesized and Alt1 models under all three
criteria. Under exact LFO-CV, LogTrial remained clearly better than the
Hypothesized model (\(\Delta \mathrm{ELPD} = 78.12\), SE = 28.92).
Relative to Alt1, its advantage was smaller but still significant
(\(\Delta \mathrm{ELPD} = 39.54\), SE = 19.20).

The more consequential comparison is Alt1 versus the Hypothesized model,
because this was the contrast on which the original model-selection
conclusion depended. Under WAIC and PSIS-LOO, Alt1 outperformed the
Hypothesized model (\(\Delta \mathrm{ELPD} = 79.7\) and 77.5,
respectively). Under exact LFO-CV, however, this difference was reduced
to \(\Delta \mathrm{ELPD} = 38.57\) with SE = 33.80. Alt1 therefore
remained directionally preferred, but its predictive advantage was small
relative to its uncertainty once the comparison was made with a
temporally appropriate criterion. The original conclusion that Alt1
should be selected over the Hypothesized model was thus not strongly
supported.

The stacking weights tell the same story. In the three-model PSIS-LOO
comparison, Alt1 received zero weight, whereas LogTrial received 82.3\%
and the Hypothesized model 17.7\% (Table~\ref{tbl-waic-loo}, B). In the
pairwise comparison with the Hypothesized model, Alt1 received 70.1\% of
the stacking weight (Table~\ref{tbl-waic-loo}, C). Thus, Alt1 is more
likely than the Hypothesized model to be the better predictive
specification, but the advantage is limited, and it disappears as a
serious competitor once LogTrial model is included.

\subsection{LFO Per-Block Comparisons}\label{lfo-per-block-comparisons}

Figure~\ref{fig-lfo} shows the per-block \(\Delta \mathrm{ELPD}\) values
from exact LFO-CV. The LogTrial model outperformed the Hypothesized
model in 13 of the 15 prediction steps. Relative to Alt1, LogTrial
performed better in 12 blocks, tied in 1, and performed worse in 2,
yielding a smaller but still positive aggregate advantage.

The Alt1 versus Hypothesized comparison was weaker. Alt1 performed
better in 11 blocks, tied in 1, and performed worse in 3, but much of
its cumulative advantage was offset by the Hypothesized model's
especially strong performance in block 10. The blockwise pattern is
therefore consistent with the aggregate LFO-CV result: Alt1 tended to
perform better, but not by a margin that was clearly separated from its
uncertainty.

Including the learning-curve term also reduced two other coefficients in
the performance equation: the autoregressive effect on performance,
\(\alpha^{P}\), decreased from 0.596 in Alt1 to 0.500 in LogTrial, and
the GPD effect on performance, \(\beta^{GP \to P}\), decreased from
0.428 to 0.369. By contrast, most other parameters remained stable
across models. The success-failure asymmetry in goal revision
(\(\beta^{GP \to G \mid S} = 0.273\);
\(\beta^{GP \to G \mid F} = 0.491\)) changed very little from Alt1 to
LogTrial, as did the GPD effect on emotions
(\(\beta^{GP \to E} \approx 0.128\)) and the emotion effect on goals,
\(\beta^{E \to G}\). The main change introduced by log(trial),
therefore, was concentrated in the performance equation: once practice
was modeled directly, the previously negative emotion-to-performance
association was markedly weakened, while a substantial learning effect
was estimated instead.

\section{Discussion}\label{discussion}

\subsection{The Emotion-to-Performance Pathway: An Omitted Learning
Curve in the Performance
Equation}\label{the-emotion-to-performance-pathway-an-omitted-learning-curve-in-the-performance-equation}

The main implication of this reanalysis is that the negative
emotion-to-performance effect reported by Lin et al.
(\citeproc{ref-lin2026dynami}{2026}) was not robust to a minimal and
substantively plausible learning-curve specification. In the LogTrial
model, \(\beta^{E \to P}\) was sharply attenuated and no longer credibly
different from zero, while a strong positive learning curve in
performance emerged (\(\beta^{\log t}_P = 0.326\)). Holding the other
predictors constant, this learning-curve term implies that participants
were expected to improve by roughly one unit of performance from the
beginning to the end of the task, corresponding to approximately one
additional correct response on the original count scale. Once this
practice-related improvement was modeled directly, the empirical support
for a negative effect of emotions on subsequent performance largely
disappeared.

This substantially weakens the original coasting interpretation. We do
not argue that the coasting hypothesis is theoretically wrong.
Experimental evidence suggests that positive emotions can, under some
conditions, reduce effort (\citeproc{ref-seo2021feelin}{Seo \& Patall,
2021}; \citeproc{ref-thurmer2020mechan}{Thürmer et al., 2020}). Our
claim is narrower and empirical: Study 1 does not provide robust
evidence for coasting once a practice-related learning curve is included
in the performance equation. The negative emotion-to-performance pathway
is better understood as a specification-dependent result than as strong
evidence that positive emotions harm later performance.

The pattern of parameter change also helps localize where the confound
operated. The coefficients that changed materially were all in the
performance equation: the emotion effect weakened, the autoregressive
coefficient decreased, and the GPD effect on performance decreased. By
contrast, the time trend in emotions was negligible, and the broader
self-regulation parameters changed very little. This is exactly the
pattern one would expect if the original performance equation were
absorbing variance from a systematic across-trial improvement that had
not been modeled explicitly. In other words, the core self-regulation
dynamics proposed by Lin et al. (\citeproc{ref-lin2026dynami}{2026}),
including the role of the GPD in goal revision and emotional response,
are not challenged by our reanalysis. What changes is specifically the
interpretation of the emotion-to-performance pathway in Study 1.

\subsection{Competing Influences on the Emotion-to-Performance
Pathway}\label{competing-influences-on-the-emotion-to-performance-pathway}

If the negative \(\beta^{E \to P}\) in Study 1 was an artifact, the sign
reversal between Studies 1 and 2 reported by Lin et al.
(\citeproc{ref-lin2026dynami}{2026}) may not reflect opposing emotional
mechanisms at all. Instead, the estimated \(\beta^{E \to P}\) in any
given study may reflect the net balance of at least two competing
influences: a genuine facilitative effect of positive emotions on
performance, consistent with the broader achievement emotions literature
(\citeproc{ref-fredrickson2001role}{Fredrickson, 2001};
\citeproc{ref-pekrun2006contro}{Pekrun, 2006}), and a practice-related
confound that pushes the estimate in the negative direction when a
learning curve in performance is unmodeled.

The relative strength of these two influences likely varies with design
features. In Study 2, several characteristics of the task may have
tilted the balance toward the genuine affective process. The exam
carried high personal stakes, plausibly strengthening the motivational
relevance of emotions for subsequent effort. The learning content varied
across sessions, reducing the scope for a smooth, monotonic practice
curve. The temporal spacing of observations over days and weeks further
limited within-session accumulation of practice effects. Under such
conditions, the practice-related confound would be weaker, allowing a
genuine positive emotion-to-performance link to be expressed in the
estimate.

Conversely, in a low-stakes repetitive task like Study 1, the genuine
affective contribution to performance may be small to begin with: when
the task carries little personal significance, how one feels may matter
less for how one performs. At the same time, the conditions for a strong
practice curve are ideal. The combination of a weak genuine effect and a
strong confound would produce a negative \(\beta^{E \to P}\) that
disappears, as we observed, once the confound is controlled.

This framework suggests that the emotion-to-performance pathway may be
most interpretable in designs where practice-related structure in
performance is either inherently weak or explicitly modeled. In highly
repetitive paradigms, any estimated \(\beta^{E \to P}\) should be
treated with caution unless a learning-curve term is included as a
robustness check. We note that this account is speculative and should be
tested by applying explicit time trends to designs that vary
systematically in task repetitiveness and personal relevance.\footnote{We
  could not test this prediction on Study 2 of Lin et al.
  (\citeproc{ref-lin2026dynami}{2026}) because the data were not
  publicly available at the time of analysis.}

\subsection{Model Comparison and Temporal
Validation}\label{model-comparison-and-temporal-validation}

A second contribution of this reanalysis concerns model comparison. The
issue is not simply that Lin et al. (\citeproc{ref-lin2026dynami}{2026})
relied on one criterion rather than several; if a single criterion is
well matched to the data structure, that can be entirely defensible. The
problem here is that the chosen criterion, WAIC, was itself
diagnostically unstable in these data, and it was applied in a setting
with strong temporal ordering.

That matters empirically. The original preference for Alt1 over the
Hypothesized model was already modest under WAIC and PSIS-LOO, and under
exact LFO-CV it became small relative to its uncertainty. The LogTrial
model was preferred to both alternatives under all criteria, although
its advantage over Alt1 was notably smaller under LFO-CV than under
PSIS-LOO, consistent with the expected reduction in optimistic bias when
future information leakage is eliminated. The substantive conclusion
thus depends not only on which model is fit, but also on whether the
validation criterion respects the temporal structure of the data.

The broader lesson is about criterion-data alignment. In longitudinal
autoregressive settings, approximate criteria such as WAIC and PSIS-LOO
may be informative, but they should not be treated as decisive when
their diagnostics are poor or when the data have temporal structure that
the criterion does not fully respect.

\subsection{Implications for Computational Modeling of
SRL}\label{implications-for-computational-modeling-of-srl}

These results have broader implications for computational modeling in
self-regulated learning. First, autoregressive structure does not by
itself solve the problem of omitted temporal structure. All three models
in this study were autoregressive, yet the performance equation still
benefited substantially from an explicit learning-curve term. Lagged
outcomes capture local persistence, but they do not necessarily capture
a global trend driven by practice, habituation, fatigue, or task
familiarization. In repeated-measures SRL designs, omitting such
structure can distort the interpretation of cross-lagged pathways.

Second, the present case highlights a form of temporal confounding that
is distinct from the stable between-person confounds emphasized in
recent methodological work on longitudinal models
(\citeproc{ref-murayama2025thinking}{\textbf{murayama2025thinking?}}).
Here, the problem was not an unobserved stable trait, but a systematic
process tied to measurement order. Such confounding can arise even in
well-specified hierarchical autoregressive models if the repeated-task
structure itself generates a substantial learning curve in one or more
of the modeled outcomes.

Third, the present findings argue for a closer connection between
substantive theory and validation strategy. When the theoretical
question concerns prediction from temporally ordered data, the preferred
model-comparison criterion should preserve that ordering. Otherwise,
cross-lagged parameters may appear more decisive than they are.

\subsection{Limitations}\label{limitations}

Several limitations should be noted. First, the learning curve was
modeled as a population-level log(trial) effect rather than allowing it
to vary across individuals. This choice was made for reasons of
parsimony and computational stability. Preliminary attempts to estimate
random log(trial) slopes led to convergence problems, likely because the
number of time points per person was limited relative to the added
individual-level complexity. The fixed-effect specification was
nevertheless sufficient for the present purpose, namely to test whether
a simple learning-curve term altered the key substantive conclusion.

Second, exact LFO-CV is computationally demanding. In our application,
with 20 blocks and 15 refits, the cost was manageable (approximately 8
hours per model), but the same approach would become burdensome for
substantially longer time series. More efficient alternatives exist in
principle. Bürkner et al. (\citeproc{ref-burkner2020approx}{2020})
proposed a PSIS approximation to LFO that avoids most refits, but that
method was developed for comparatively simple univariate time-series
settings and is not feasible for our jointly autoregressive hierarchical
model, where each prediction step adds a large block of new observations
and the posterior shift is too large for stable importance sampling.
Sequential Monte Carlo methods could in principle recover the
one-step-ahead predictive densities required for LFO as a byproduct of a
single sequential run, but standard particle-based approaches face
severe weight degeneracy in parameter spaces of the scale considered
here (approximately 4,000+ parameters). Developing scalable
approximations to LFO for complex hierarchical autoregressive models
therefore remains an important methodological task.

Third, our empirical reanalysis was limited to Study 1. The
interpretation offered for Study 2 is theoretically plausible and
generates a clear prediction, but it remains to be tested directly once
those data become available.

Fourth, the log(trial) term captures a global nonlinear learning curve
but does not identify the underlying mechanism. The observed improvement
may reflect practice, familiarity with the interface, procedural
adaptation, or some combination of these. Our claim is about omitted
learning-curve structure in the performance equation, not about a
uniquely identified psychological process.

\subsection{Conclusion}\label{conclusion}

Our reanalysis supports the broader dynamic framework proposed by Lin et
al. (\citeproc{ref-lin2026dynami}{2026}): goals, performance, and
emotions are interrelated over time, and the goal-performance
discrepancy remains central to that system. However, the specific claim
that positive emotions negatively affect subsequent performance in Study
1 is not robust once a practice-related learning curve is modeled
explicitly. The negative emotion-to-performance coefficient depends on
how performance change over trials is specified, and it is substantially
reduced once the model is given a direct way to represent across-trial
improvement. This reinterpretation also offers a parsimonious account of
the otherwise puzzling sign reversal between Studies 1 and 2: rather
than opposite emotional mechanisms, the two studies may differ in the
balance between a genuine facilitative role of emotions and a
practice-related confound that depends on design features such as task
repetitiveness, stakes, and temporal spacing.

\section{Model Specifications}\label{apx-a}

This appendix provides the full mathematical specification of the three
models compared in this study. All models share the same hierarchical
Bayesian structure and differ only in the specification of the
conditional means for goals, performance, and emotions. We first
describe the common elements, then present the model-specific equations.

\subsection{Common Structure}\label{common-structure}

At each time point \(t\) (blocks 2 through 20), goals (\(G\)),
performance (\(P\)), and emotions (\(E\)) are modeled as jointly
normally distributed, conditional on the previous time point:

\[
G_{t+1} \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu^G_{t+1},\; \sigma_G), \quad
P_{t+1} \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu^P_{t+1},\; \sigma_P), \quad
E_{t+1} \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu^E_{t+1},\; \sigma_E)
\tag{A1}
\]

where \(\sigma_G\), \(\sigma_P\), and \(\sigma_E\) are population-level
residual standard deviations. Block 1 values serve as initial conditions
and are not modeled. Each parameter \(\theta\) entering the conditional
means below is specified hierarchically via a non-centered
parameterization: \(\theta_i = \bar{\theta} + \sigma_\theta \cdot z_i\),
where \(z_i \sim \mathcal{N}(0, 1)\),
\(\bar{\theta} \sim \mathcal{N}(0, 3)\), and
\(\sigma_\theta \sim \mathcal{N}^+(0, 3)\). Subscript \(i\) indexes
participants.

\subsection{Conditional Means}\label{conditional-means}

The performance and emotion equations are shared across the Hypothesized
and Alt1 models:

\[
\mu^P_{t+1} = \gamma^P_i + \alpha^P_i \, P_t + \beta^{GP \to P}_i \,(G_{t+1} - P_t) + \beta^{E \to P}_i \, E_t \tag{A2}
\]

\[
\mu^E_{t+1} = \gamma^E_i + \alpha^E_i \, E_t + \beta^{GP \to E}_i \,(P_{t+1} - G_{t+1}) \tag{A3}
\]

The three models differ in the goal equation and in whether a log-trial
trend is included, as described below.

\textbf{Hypothesized Model.} The goal equation uses a single symmetric
coefficient for the goal-performance discrepancy (GPD):

\[
\mu^G_{t+1} = \gamma^G_i + \alpha^G_i \, G_t + \beta^{GP \to G}_i \,(P_t - G_t) + \beta^{E \to G}_i \, E_t \tag{A4}
\]

\textbf{Alt1 Model.} The goal equation replaces the symmetric GPD term
with separate coefficients for success and failure:

\[
\mu^G_{t+1} = \gamma^G_i + \alpha^G_i \, G_t + \beta^{E \to G}_i \, E_t +
\begin{cases}
\beta^{GP \to G|S}_i\,(P_t - G_t) & \text{if } P_t > G_t \text{ (success)} \\
\beta^{GP \to G|F}_i \,(P_t - G_t) & \text{if } P_t \leq G_t \text{ (failure)}
\end{cases}
\tag{A5}
\]

\textbf{LogTrial Model.} This model extends Alt1 by adding
population-level log-trial effects to all three conditional means. The
goal equation is identical to Equation A5 with an additional term
\(\beta^{\log t}_G \log(t+1)\). The performance and emotion equations
become:

\[
\mu^P_{t+1} = \gamma^P_i + \alpha^P_i \, P_t + \beta^{GP \to P}_i \,(G_{t+1} - P_t) + \beta^{E \to P}_i \, E_t + \beta^{\log t}_P \log(t+1) \tag{A6}
\]

\[
\mu^E_{t+1} = \gamma^E_i + \alpha^E_i \, E_t + \beta^{GP \to E}_i \,(P_{t+1} - G_{t+1}) + \beta^{\log t}_E \log(t+1) \tag{A7}
\]

The three log-trial coefficients (\(\beta^{\log t}_G\),
\(\beta^{\log t}_P\), \(\beta^{\log t}_E\)) are population-level only,
with no individual-level variation. This choice was made to maintain
parsimony; see Limitations for discussion.
